# Intro to Specialty Coffee: A Reading Guide

*Adapted from "Intro to Specialty Coffee | A 'Third Wave' Coffee Overview" by Riley Westbrook, co-founder of Valor Coffee*

## What Makes Coffee "Specialty"?

Specialty coffee isn't defined by price or brew method — plenty of specialty coffee is affordable and brewed on automatic machines, while expensive, manually-brewed coffee can still fail to qualify. At its core, specialty coffee represents a shift from coffee-as-commodity to coffee-as-a-nuanced, traceable experience.

The industry benchmark comes from the Specialty Coffee Association: coffees are scored across metrics like flavor, balance, and acidity, and a score of **80 or above** earns the "specialty" label. Specialty-grade coffee typically also has strong traceability back to its source, since it isn't sold at commodity prices to a local mill.

One of the appealing things about specialty coffee is that it's an "affordable luxury" — you can access exceptional, boundary-pushing coffee for the price of a café drink, unlike luxury categories such as bourbon or sports cars that require serious money to reach the top tier.

## The Three Waves of Coffee

| Wave | Era | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| **First Wave** | Early–late 1900s | Coffee as commodity (Folgers, Mr. Coffee); grab-and-go, little thought given to quality |
| **Second Wave** | Later 1900s | Marked by Starbucks; introduced espresso culture, drink customization, and coffee shops as a "third place" |
| **Third Wave** | Today | Coffee as craft — full traceability, labels detailing altitude/variety/processing, barista pride in technique |

## Where Coffee Comes From

Coffee grows in the "coffee belt" — tropical regions with the right soil and climate, spanning Mexico down through Brazil and Colombia, and across parts of Africa like Ethiopia and Kenya. **Altitude** matters a great deal: higher-grown coffee is harder to produce but tends to yield denser, more flavorful beans. Harvest seasons vary by country, which is why roaster menus rotate through the year.

**Rough origin flavor associations** (broad generalizations, not hard rules):
- **Ethiopia** — floral, tea-like (the legendary birthplace of coffee)
- **Colombia** — extremely varied; can range from Ethiopia-like to Brazil-like; a hub for processing experimentation
- **Brazil** — nutty, chocolatey
- **Kenya** — bright, acidic
- **Costa Rica** — sweet, chocolatey, citrusy; early leader in honey processing
- **Guatemala** — chocolatey, phosphoric-acid-forward from volcanic soil

## Variety, Traceability, and Labeling

Beyond origin, coffee **varieties** (genetic variations, like different apple cultivars) shape flavor. **Gesha** is the best-known example — an expensive, slow-to-fruit variety known for wild, distinctive flavors.

Coffee labels typically follow a hierarchy from broad to specific: country → region → producer/farm. The more specific and traceable the label, the better the coffee tends to be — though this isn't an absolute rule.

**Organic certification** is expensive, and many farmers grow coffee using organic-adjacent practices without paying for the certification. Organic status is not a reliable indicator of flavor quality.

## Processing: How the Cherry Becomes the Bean

A coffee cherry has layers: skin/pulp → mucilage (the sticky "honey") → parchment → silver skin → the green bean itself. Most cherries hold two beans; a cherry with just one is called a **peaberry**. Processing determines when and how these layers are removed, which drives fermentation levels and flavor.

- **Washed** — Fruit is removed down to the parchment before drying. Produces clean, classic-tasting cups with minimal fermentation character.
- **Natural** — Cherries are dried whole with the fruit still on, producing heavy fermentation and fruity, wine-like flavors. Often a gateway to more adventurous specialty coffee.
- **Honey** — A middle ground: skin and pulp are removed but the mucilage stays on during drying. Sweet, less wild than natural. "Yellow/red/black honey" refers to how much fruit residue was left on.
- **Experimental methods** — Anaerobic (fermented without oxygen), co-ferments (fermented with other ingredients, like watermelon), and carbonic maceration (CO2-flushed tanks) all push flavor into new territory.

The same coffee, from the same farm, processed two different ways can taste completely different — and more intensive processing generally costs more due to added labor.

## Single Origin vs. Blends

**Single origin** coffee traces to one country, and can go even deeper: nano lot → micro lot → farm/estate → co-op level. Co-ops pool coffee from multiple small farms, giving farmers without their own processing equipment a route to market — a co-op coffee isn't automatically lower quality.

**Direct trade** means a roaster buys straight from producers; it's a practice, not an official certification. **Fair trade**, by contrast, is an official certification that sets a price floor — most specialty roasters already pay well above it.

**Blends** combine coffees from different origins, varieties, or processing methods. Well-made modern blends aren't the "kitchen sink" commodity blends of the past — many roasters blend two excellent, traceable coffees to create a new flavor profile, often chocolatey and round, and well-suited to espresso with milk.

## Brew Methods

- **Percolation** (drip, V60) — water passes through a bed of grounds, usually via paper filter, producing clean, bright cups.
- **Immersion** — grounds steep in water and are filtered out (often with metal), producing thicker, silkier cups with more retained oils.
- **Espresso** — a brew method (not a roast level) where pressurized water is forced through finely-ground, tamped coffee, producing a concentrated shot that forms the base of milk drinks.
- **Cupping** — the worldwide industry-standard tasting protocol: grind, add water, break the crust, taste with a spoon. Used daily by roasters to make purchasing decisions and maintain a consistent quality standard globally.
- **Batch brew** shouldn't be dismissed as inferior to manual pour-over — its main strength is *consistency*, which matters enormously in a busy café.

## Grinders and Machines

- **Burr grinders only** — blade grinders create inconsistent grind size, which undermines extraction.
- **Conical burrs** tend to favor round, thick, creamy coffee; **flat burrs** tend to favor bright, modern, acidic coffee.
- **Single-boiler espresso machines** share one boiler between brewing and steaming, requiring temperature switching. **Dual-boiler machines** separate the two for smoother workflow.
- **Pressure profiling** (on more advanced machines) lets you vary pressure through the shot for more flavor control, usually at a higher price point.

## Common Espresso-Based Drinks

| Drink | Composition |
|---|---|
| Long black / Americano | Espresso + water (varying ratios) |
| Macchiato | Espresso + a small dash of milk (~1 oz) |
| Cortado | Roughly equal parts espresso and steamed milk |
| Flat white | ~5–6 oz total, espresso + steamed milk, thin microfoam |
| Cappuccino | ~5–6 oz total, traditionally more air/foam than a flat white |
| Latte | Espresso + a larger volume of steamed milk; a catch-all for bigger milk drinks |

## Roast Levels

There's no industry-wide standard for what counts as "light," "medium," or "dark" — one roaster's light roast can be another's dark roast. Specialty roasters generally roast lighter overall than commodity coffee, stopping well before **second crack** (an audible popping stage after which beans turn oily and take on heavy carbon/smoky flavors). Pushing coffee too dark masks the bean's natural origin character with generic "roast flavor."

**Roast dates matter, but not the way many beginners assume.** Freshly roasted coffee is full of CO2 gas, and brewing it too soon (within a couple of days) can produce off flavors. A rest period of **7–10 days** post-roast is generally recommended, and many coffees still taste great 30+ days off roast.

## The Three-Drink Test

To judge a new café: order an **espresso**, a **cappuccino**, and a **filter brew**. Together they reveal how well a shop dials in espresso, manages milk texture, and handles a straightforward black cup.

## Key Vocabulary

- **Acidity** — brightness/citrus character, literally measurable on the pH scale; not inherently negative, though it can be dialed too high for some palates.
- **Extraction** — how much flavor/compound is pulled from the grounds into the brew. Under-extraction reads as sour/puckering; over-extraction reads as bitter with a lingering finish.
- **Dialing in** — the ongoing process of adjusting brew parameters (grind size especially) while tasting, to hit a target recipe (input, output, time).
- **Body** — mouthfeel; can be adjusted by changing brew ratios (e.g., a shorter espresso pull feels thicker/creamier).
- **Crema** — the top layer on espresso, caused by trapped gas from fresh coffee; not flavorful on its own.
- **WDT (Weiss Distribution Technique)** — using fine needles to break up clumps in the espresso puck and prevent channeling.
- **Channeling** — uneven paths of water through the coffee bed, causing simultaneous over- and under-extraction.
- **RDT (Ross Droplet Technique)** — lightly misting beans with water before grinding to reduce static.
- **TDS / EY (Total Dissolved Solids / Extraction Yield)** — measurements taken with a refractometer to objectively quantify how a coffee was extracted.
- **Terroir** — how environment, soil, and farm conditions shape a bean's flavor.

## Common Myths, Debunked

1. **Dark roast doesn't have more caffeine than light roast** (and vice versa) — it roughly evens out.
2. **Espresso doesn't have more caffeine than drip** — a 12 oz drip cup usually has more total caffeine than a double shot; espresso is just more *concentrated*.
3. **Expensive doesn't always mean better** — terroir and sourcing matter more than price tag; marketing plays a large role in what people believe about quality.
4. **You don't need an expensive setup to start** — build your gear collection gradually (grinder → pour-over device → scale → kettle) rather than buying everything at once.

## Closing Takeaways

- Explore broadly: try different origins, processing methods, roasters, and both single origins and blends.
- Your palate preferences are valid even if they diverge from industry or professional norms — this is meant to be enjoyable, not stressful.
- Intentionally brewing "wrong" (under- and over-extracting on purpose) is a legitimate way to train your palate and discover your preferences.
